“In the United States, among the important forces in the convergence of per capita income levels ... has been the inter-regional migration of persons.”
-- (), 1880

“Advanced and expanding regions are usually the receiving regions for internal migration.”
-- (), 2asf

In lieu of the notes above, we attempted to observe relationship between income and migration in metropolitan areas in the United States using migration and income data between 2013 and 2017 from the U.S. Census. Not seeing as large of an association as we expected, we decided to modify our objectives. In addition to visualizing migration data, we wanted to explore reasons why specific metropolitan areas either diverged from or followed our hypothesis, highlighting interesting historical and economic events around outliers.
Scroll down to explore!

THE BEGINNING

For each metropolitan area, the figure to the right plots the relationship between the net population change ratio (net number of individuals who migrated to or from the area divided by the total population) and real personal income (RPI, which is normalized in light of inflation), between 2013 and 2017. As you can probably perceive, there is no clear relationship between the two. However, we’ve identified outliers, which are circled in dotted lines!

AN INTERACTIVE

We’ve encoded the four outliers indicated in scatterplot into a map of the United States.

  • Mouse over any metropolitan area to view inflow and outflow data (Only the magnitudes of the top four flows are displayed)
  • Click on an outlier to read more about the metropolitan area and economic events surrounding it
